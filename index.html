Ashlar

Ashlar is a font that is designed with the identity of physical spaces on the digital world. With a growing demand to move everything to a digitial form, the digital space and identity of physical art spaces are also created. To contrast and stand a part from the majority of the branding that is currently being used in other art spaces, Ashlar is a thin, medium contrast font that also has a variable axis of width to fill any space.

Features

Ashlar gives an air of stability and elegance thanks to its vertical stress and smooth stress modulation. Designed with classic transitional typeface principles, Ashlar takes on the strength of modern type but reamains with the classical and handmade aesthetic of the old style fonts.

The serifs for Ashlar provide a unique characteristic to the font. The angled exaggerated serifs give a sharp and dramatic look, giving a strong sense of direction. Giving these forms intention and an interesting pattern when typeset.

With a higher x-height and an open counters, Ashlar is inspired by light-weight display serifs and the open spaces in museums. The contrast between the thickest and thinest sections of the font are drastic but maintain the thin and delicate forms by choosing lower values.

Mona Hatoum

born Beirut, Lebanon, 1952
residing in London, United Kingdom

Densely black and light-absorbing, Mona Hatoum’s sculpture Socle du Monde (1992–93), physically dominated the gallery space. Paying explicit homage to the work of Piero Manzoni, it also related to the aesthetic of Eva Hesse and her subversion of the cold industrial forms of Minimalism. While the underlying structure is an imposing cube constructed of mild steel (not unlike the kind of Minimalist works presented by artists such as Robert Morris and Richard Serra), Hatoum coated it in a material that adhered to the metal surface, gathering in meandering channels to create patterns that seem organic and alive, resembling, perhaps, rippling intestines or sea urchins.
